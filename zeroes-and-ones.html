Zeroes and Ones

Understanding the Binary Number System

The Origins of Number Systems

Our amazing computer age started with a simple idea whose origins date back to ancient Babylon and Egypt. The Babylonians developed the first positional number system using the rather large base of 60. Our numbers are based on the same idea but employ the smaller base ten.

In base 10 the number 453.25 means:

453.25 = 4 × 100 + 5 × 10 + 3 × 1 + 2 × (1/10) + 5 × (1/100)

Notice that all the numbers in bold are powers of 10. They get larger by a factor of 10 as we move to the left, or, alternatively, get smaller by a factor of 10 as we move to the right. This approach enables us to write any number, no matter how large or how small, using just 10 digits. It also allows us to add, subtract, multiply, and divide numbers with relative ease.

In base 10 we use powers of 10. In base 2 we will use powers of 2.

Egyptian Multiplication

Egyptian priests developed a clever multiplication algorithm that involved doubling. They expressed the smaller number as a sum of powers of two, that is 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and so on.

Example: 7 × 13

7 = 1 + 2 + 4

7 × 13 = (1 + 2 + 4) × 13 = 1 × 13 + 2 × 13 + 4 × 13 = 13 + 26 + 52 = 91

The Egyptians were unknowingly using the binary structure of numbers. Any positive integer can be written as a sum of distinct powers of two. This idea is exactly what the binary system formalizes.

Decimal Number Powers of 2 Sum
5 4 + 1 2² + 2⁰
13 8 + 4 + 1 2³ + 2² + 2⁰
20 16 + 4 2⁴ + 2²
37 32 + 4 + 1 2⁵ + 2² + 2⁰

Some powers of two are skipped. In binary representation each power of two is used at most once, which corresponds to writing digits 0 or 1.

In fact, every positive integer can be written uniquely as a sum of distinct powers of two. That is why each whole number has exactly one binary representation.

The Binary System

This extraordinary fact prompted people to use two as a base of a number system. It is called the binary system. To express any number, we only need ones and zeros. We place them in columns whose place values are powers of two, just as in our decimal (base 10) number system we use powers of 10.

Example: 110010 in binary means:

1 × 32 + 1 × 16 + 0 × 8 + 0 × 4 + 1 × 2 + 0 × 1 = 32 + 16 + 2 = 50

The fact that there are only two digits makes binary arithmetic suitable for computers. A circuit has exactly two states: it is either on or off. Computers recognize on as 1 and off as 0.

Notice what happens when we add 1 to 111: all the 1s turn into 0s, and a new 1 appears on the left. This is the binary version of carrying.

Converting Binary to Decimal

To convert a binary number to decimal, multiply each digit by its place value (power of 2) and sum the results.

Example: Convert 110110 to decimal

Place values from right to left: 2⁰, 2¹, 2², 2³, 2⁴, 2⁵

1 × 2⁵ + 1 × 2⁴ + 0 × 2³ + 1 × 2² + 1 × 2¹ + 0 × 2⁰

= 1 × 32 + 1 × 16 + 0 × 8 + 1 × 4 + 1 × 2 + 0 × 1

= 32 + 16 + 0 + 4 + 2 + 0 = 54

So, 110110 in binary is 54 in decimal.

Converting Decimal to Binary

One standard method is to divide by two repeatedly until the quotient becomes 0.

Example: Convert 43 to binary by repeated division

1
43 ÷ 2 = 21 remainder 1
2
21 ÷ 2 = 10 remainder 1
3
10 ÷ 2 = 5 remainder 0
4
5 ÷ 2 = 2 remainder 1
5
2 ÷ 2 = 1 remainder 0
6
1 ÷ 2 = 0 remainder 1

Result: Reading the remainders from bottom to top: 101011

Another useful method: largest powers of two

Many people find it easier to build a number out of powers of two.

43 = 32 + 8 + 2 + 1

So the places for 32, 8, 2, and 1 get a 1, and the others get a 0.

Therefore, 43 = 101011₂

This method connects directly to the Egyptian doubling idea from earlier in the lesson.

Binary Addition

Binary addition follows simple rules:

0 + 0 = 0

1 + 0 = 1

0 + 1 = 1

1 + 1 = 10 (0 with a carry of 1)

This is just like decimal addition where 9 + 1 = 10. In both systems, when we run out of digits in one place, we carry to the next place.

Example: Add 110 and 101

  110
+ 101
-----
1011

Explanation:

  • Ones column: 0 + 1 = 1
  • Twos column: 1 + 0 = 1
  • Fours column: 1 + 1 = 10, so we write 0 and carry 1
  • Eights column: the carry gives us 1

Result: 1011 in binary, which is 11 in decimal.

Bits and Bytes

A binary digit is called a bit.

bit = binary digit

8 bits = 1 byte

With 8 bits, the smallest number is 00000000₂ = 0 and the largest number is 11111111₂ = 255.

This is why in many computing contexts, such as colors, values often range from 0 to 255.
