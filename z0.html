Zeroes and Ones

Understanding the Binary Number System

The Origins of Number Systems

Our amazing computer age started with a simple idea whose origins date back to ancient Babylon and Egypt. The Babylonians developed the first positional number system using the rather large base of 60. Our numbers are based on the same idea but employ the smaller base - ten.

In base 10 the number 453.25 means:

453.25 = 4 × 100 + 5 × 10 + 3 × 1 + 2 × (1/10) + 5 × (1/100)

Notice that all the numbers in bold are powers of 10. They get larger by a factor of 10 as we move to the left, or, alternatively, get smaller by a factor of 10 as we move to the right. This approach enables us to write any number, no matter how large or how small, using just 10 digits. It also allows us to add, subtract, multiply, and divide numbers with relative ease. We can borrow and carry using these four operations.

Egyptian Multiplication

Egyptian priests developed a clever multiplication algorithm that involved doubling. They expressed the smaller number as a sum of powers of two, that is 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and so on.

Example: 7 × 13

7 = 1 + 2 + 4

7 × 13 = (1 + 2 + 4) × 13 = 1 × 13 + 2 × 13 + 4 × 13 = 13 + 26 + 52 = 91

The Egyptians figured out that any number can be written as a sum of powers of two:

Decimal Number Powers of 2 Sum
5 4 + 1 2² + 2⁰
13 8 + 4 + 1 2³ + 2² + 2⁰
20 16 + 4 2⁴ + 2²
37 32 + 4 + 1 2⁵ + 2² + 2⁰

Notice that some powers of two are skipped, but they are never multiplied (unlike in our base 10 system, where 35 = 3 × 10 + 5 × 1).

The Binary System

This extraordinary fact prompted people to use two as a base of a number system. It is called the binary system. To express any number, we only need ones and zeros! We place them in the columns whose place values are the powers of two, just as we did in our decimal (base 10) number system.

Example: 110010 in binary means:

1 × 32 + 1 × 16 + 0 × 8 + 0 × 4 + 1 × 2 + 0 × 1 = 32 + 16 + 2 = 50

The fact that there are only two digits makes binary arithmetic suitable for computers. A circuit has exactly two states: it is either on or off. Computers recognize on as 1 and off as 0.

Converting Decimal to Binary

To convert a number to binary we divide by two repeatedly until we obtain a quotient of 0.

Example: Convert 43 to binary

1
43 ÷ 2 = 21 remainder 1
2
21 ÷ 2 = 10 remainder 1
3
10 ÷ 2 = 5 remainder 0
4
5 ÷ 2 = 2 remainder 1
5
2 ÷ 2 = 1 remainder 0
6
1 ÷ 2 = 0 remainder 1

Result: Reading the remainders from bottom to top: 101011

So, 43 in decimal is 101011 in binary.

Converting Binary to Decimal

To convert a binary number to decimal, we multiply each digit by its place value (power of 2) and sum the results.

Example: Convert 110110 to decimal

Place values from right to left: 2⁰, 2¹, 2², 2³, 2⁴, 2⁵

1 × 2⁵ + 1 × 2⁴ + 0 × 2³ + 1 × 2² + 1 × 2¹ + 0 × 2⁰

= 1 × 32 + 1 × 16 + 0 × 8 + 1 × 4 + 1 × 2 + 0 × 1

= 32 + 16 + 0 + 4 + 2 + 0 = 54

So, 110110 in binary is 54 in decimal.

Binary Addition

Binary addition follows simple rules:

0 + 0 = 0

1 + 0 = 1

0 + 1 = 1

1 + 1 = 10 (0 with a carry of 1)

Example: Add 110 and 101

1 1 0 (carry)
  1 1 0
+  1 0 1
--------
1 0 1 1

Explanation:

  • Ones column: 0 + 1 = 1
  • Twos column: 1 + 0 = 1
  • Fours column: 1 + 1 = 10 (write 0, carry 1 to eights column)
  • Eights column: 1 (carry) = 1

Result: 1011 in binary (which is 11 in decimal)
